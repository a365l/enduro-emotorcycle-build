# Phase 1 — Donor Bikes & Preowned Repairs

## Overview

Phase 1 was about stretching the budget and sourcing better parts through flipping. Two crashed donor bikes were purchased, stripped for usable components, and the remainder sold. A broken motor and battery were also bought cheaply with the intention of repairing and reselling to fund the next stage. A Sabvaton controller was sourced secondhand — it arrived dead, was diagnosed with shorted MOSFETs, and a repair attempt was made but ultimately failed.

## Sabvaton Controller — Dead on Arrival

A Sabvaton controller was bought secondhand to replace the generic Phase 0 controller. On arrival, continuity was found between the positive and negative battery terminals — a dead short, indicating blown MOSFETs. The controller had never been connected to the bike.

A MOSFET replacement was attempted but was not successful. The controller was written off.

## Lessons & Notes

- Finding continuity (dead short) between B+ and B- on a controller before connecting anything is the first check that should always be done on secondhand controllers — it costs nothing and takes 10 seconds with a multimeter
- Shorted MOSFETs on budget Sabvaton-style controllers are a common failure — gate driver issues or a previous over-voltage event are usually the cause
- MOSFET replacement on these controllers is feasible in principle but requires identifying which of the 18–24 FETs failed, which usually means checking each gate-to-drain individually — the repair attempt here didn't get past this stage
- Buying broken parts to flip is a valid budget strategy but comes with real time cost — the motor repair and battery work ate significant hours
- The eBike testing device is genuinely useful and worth having — far faster than bench-testing with a power supply
